**1. THE GILDED KNOT**

**HOOK:** A guilt-ridden librarian in a floating city of bone-and-silk must sacrifice her own memory of her dead son, one year at a time, to keep the city’s wind-gods tethered—but an Anchor who refuses to forget is leading a rebellion that wants her to stop.

**WORLD:** The archipelago city of Seraphine hangs in the upper atmosphere, woven from the ribs of extinct sky-whales and dyed silk that catches the perpetual twilight. Citizens live on platforms tethered to the bones; beneath, a sea of cloud roils with hungry wind-spirits. The air smells of ozone and incense. Every building chimes with bone-wind-chimes that record the names of the dead.

**MAGIC/COST:** *Anchoring* — a ritual where a mourner (the Anchor) binds a wind-spirit to the city by *gifting* them a specific memory. Each year, the Anchor must voluntarily offer another memory of their lost beloved to the spirit; the spirit feeds on it and grows stronger, keeping the city aloft. The Anchor remembers less and less of the person they loved most, until eventually they forget their face, their voice, their name. The memory is *gone forever*.

**TENSION:** Our protagonist, the chief Anchor Sadira, has tethered the city for 12 years. She has one year left before she forgets her son entirely. The cosmic conflict: if she stops Anchoring, the city falls and everyone she might someday save dies. The personal: each memory she gives is the only proof she ever had a son. The rebellion wants her to stop, claiming the Anchors are being asked to erase the dead *twice*—first from life, then from memory. But if she stops, the wind-spirits’ hunger turns ravenous and thousands die.

**THEME:** Is grief a debt we owe the dead, or a gift we owe the living? And if memory is the only immortality we have, is it moral to spend it as currency?

**WHY IT’S NOT GENERIC:** The magic is not inherited or learned at a school—it’s a *job* for grieving parents, a bureaucratic tragedy where you clock in to lose your child again, and the setting is a fragile sky-archipelago anchored by sorrow, not a European castle.
